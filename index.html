The Clustering Illusion

Formally, the clustering illusion is a cognitive bias where seemingly non-random patterns are identified within random distributions.

True randomness is an unintuitive concept, and is difficult for humans to reason about. We tend to assume that random selection results in a completely uniform distribution over the sample space, but in reality a random selection can result in any distribution.

For example, if you were to flip a coin thirty times, which result set would you expect? 

A: H T H T H H T H T T

B: H T T T T H H T H H

Most people would agree that the result B just looks less likely. In fact, I completely made up A, while B came from actual coin flips. You would think that seeing tails four times in a row is incredibly unlikely, but these small clusters occur frequently in random distributions.

A canonical example of this bias can be seen in the blitz of London during World War II. As German V-1 and V-2 rockets rained on London, maps of the missile strike locations showed certain neighbourhoods receiving much more damage than others. Gilovich, Thomas. How We Know What Isn't So. The Free Press, 1991, page 20. This led the Brits to two (incorrect) conclusions: that the German rockets were far more precise than they actually were, and that the untouched neighbourhoods housed German spies.
